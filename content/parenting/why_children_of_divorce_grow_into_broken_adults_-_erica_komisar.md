# Why Children of Divorce Grow into Broken Adults - Erica Komisar

## Analysis: Erica Komisar on Attachment, Divorce, and the Mental Health Crisis

## Narrative Summary

In this wide-ranging conversation, clinical social worker and psychoanalyst Erica Komisar delivers a bracing, evidence-rooted argument that modern society has systematically devalued the conditions children need to develop into emotionally healthy adults. Drawing on decades of clinical practice and three books, she covers the neurobiological foundations of attachment security, the psychological cost of divorce on children at different developmental stages, and the societal forces — feminism's second wave, careerism, narcissism, and broken family court systems — that have conspired against children's mental health. Her central thesis is uncomfortable but consistent: *children are not resilient by default; they are neurologically fragile, and the adults around them are responsible for building that resilience from the ground up.*

## Contextual Key Insights

### On Attachment Security & Brain Development

- **Babies are born neurologically dysregulated**, not resilient. The mother's physical and emotional presence is the primary tool for regulating a baby's cortisol, heartbeat, breath, and emotional state from birth through age three.
- **85% of the right brain develops by age three.** This window of rapid "brain architecture" is uniquely vulnerable to chronic stress, which physically reshapes the amygdala and stress-regulation systems in lasting, damaging ways.
- **The stress-regulating system is meant to be "offline" for the first year.** Skin-to-skin contact, babywearing, and physical proximity buffer the infant's cortisol, introducing stress only incrementally — which is how resilience is actually *built*, not assumed.
- **Neurological sensitivity is a real genetic trait** (a short allele on the serotonin receptor), but it is *neutralizable* by sensitive, empathic caregiving in the first three years. Without it, the same gene is *exacerbated*, predisposing those children to anxiety, depression, and behavioral dysregulation.
- **ADHD, anxiety, and depression have no identified genetic precursor** — they are symptomatic of stress overexposure, specifically a hypervigilant amygdala trapped in chronic fight-or-flight. Distractibility is the "fleeing" component of that response.
- **Attachment patterns are generationally transmitted** not genetically but through acquired environmental characteristics — anxiously attached parents reproduce anxiously attached children, not through DNA but through behavior, which *is* the environment.
- **Insecure attachment at 12 months predicts insecure attachment in 72% of cases 20 years later**, and is directly correlated with depression, anxiety, and borderline personality disorder.

### On Divorce & Children

- **Divorce is universally not good for children**, but chronic parental conflict is *worse* than a well-managed divorce. A good divorce beats a terrible marriage.
- **The worst developmental windows for divorce** are 0–3 (attachment formation) and 9–25, particularly 11–14 (early adolescence, the most emotionally turbulent period). The most *stable* window, if divorce must happen, is roughly ages 6–11.
- **Divorce before age three disrupts attachment security at its most critical phase.** If unavoidable, parents should prioritize minimizing the infant's separation from the primary attachment figure (typically the mother) over parental fairness.
- **The 50/50 custody model is developmentally uninformed.** Courts prioritize legal fairness over neurobiological reality. Splitting infant custody evenly treats children as possessions rather than as fragile developing beings.
- **Children experience divorce as grief** — following the Kübler-Ross stages (denial, sadness, anger, acceptance). Many children and parents get *stuck* in earlier stages for years or even decades.
- **Children commonly blame themselves** due to magical thinking — the developmental-stage belief that they control events around them. This is not pathological; it is age-appropriate. But it must be explicitly addressed and dispelled by parents.
- **The 2-3-2 custody schedule is particularly harmful** because it destroys the stability children need, particularly during school weeks. A primary residence with frequent access by the non-primary parent is far superior.
- **Parental alienation is real but rarer than assumed.** It typically arises in high-conflict, belligerent divorces where one parent weaponizes the child against the other. Education and emotional support reduce it.
- **College departure is not a safe window for divorce.** Children launching into adulthood need a stable "tether" to individuate from. Divorcing at 18 is highly disruptive; waiting until the child is fully launched (mid-20s) is far preferable.

### On Maternal & Paternal Roles

- **Mothers and fathers nurture differently due to different hormonal profiles.** Oxytocin in mothers (amplified by birth and breastfeeding) produces sensitive, empathic, attuned caregiving. Vasopressin in fathers produces protective-aggressive vigilance and playful, physically stimulating interaction — both important, but serving *different* developmental functions.
- **The primary attachment figure is defined by who is emotionally and physically present the majority of the time**, not by gender. If a father is the primary caregiver, he is the primary attachment figure. But most birth-giving, breastfeeding mothers remain so naturally.
- **Fathers play a critical resilience-building role** through rough-and-tumble play and separation practice — but only *after* secure attachment is established. Separation before attachment is traumatic.
- **Fathers in divorce who sacrifice short-term fairness for child-centric arrangements** produce the best long-term outcomes in Komisar's clinical experience. The children will ultimately recognize and appreciate this sacrifice.
- **Left-side cradling reflects right-brain-to-right-brain connection** and is a behavioral marker of secure maternal attachment. Postpartum depression can be partially diagnosed by observing cradling side.

### On Societal & Policy Failures

- **There is no federal paid parental leave in the United States** — arguably the most significant policy failure in the context of child mental health. Komisar advocates 12–18 months of paid leave, beginning *before* birth to reduce prenatal cortisol.
- **Daycare centers are the worst possible substitute caregiving environment** — high caregiver-to-child ratios, transient caregivers, chronic noise and overstimulation, and separation from the primary attachment figure spike infant cortisol to measurably dangerous levels. Better alternatives, in order: extended family, a dedicated nanny/surrogate caregiver at home, or a shared nanny with one or two other families.
- **The second wave of feminism devalued caregiving work** by pursuing "identification with the aggressor" — aspiring to male professional models of success rather than demanding that women's work (caregiving, nurturing, teaching) be socially and financially recognized as equal in value.
- **The mental health crisis in youth is largely traceable to disrupted early attachment, high divorce rates, and systemic undervaluation of caregiving** — not genetics, not phones alone, not social media in isolation.
- **Quality time is a rationalization, not a reality.** Emotional presence is impossible without physical presence. Children need a consistent "digestive system" — a present adult to metabolize their daily experiences.

## Step-by-Step Action Plan

### For Expectant or New Parents

1. **Prioritize the first three years above all other life planning.** Treat this period as neurologically sacred — it is literally when the brain is being architecturally built.
2. **Maximize skin-to-skin contact, babywearing, and physical proximity**, especially in the first year. The goal is to keep cortisol low, introduce stress incrementally, and be the emotional regulator for your child.
3. **Arrange finances and employment *before* the baby arrives** so that at least one parent (the primary caregiver) can remain the consistent, emotionally present attachment figure for 0–3 years.
4. **If you must use substitute care**, follow this hierarchy: extended family first → dedicated home-based nanny/surrogate caregiver → shared nanny in-home care → everything else. Avoid institutional daycare wherever possible.
5. **Sleep when the baby sleeps.** Do not use nap time to "catch up" on work, email, or chores. Your cortisol regulation matters for your child's cortisol regulation.
6. **Cradle from the left side and make sustained eye contact** during feeding, regardless of whether breastfeeding or bottle-feeding. This facilitates right-brain-to-right-brain attunement.
7. **Mirror and name your baby's emotions** rather than trying to immediately "fix" or dismiss them. Emotional reflection — "I can see you're upset" — is how regulation becomes internalized.

### For Co-Parents Navigating Divorce

1. **If possible, delay divorce until after the child's third birthday.** If there is no abuse, prioritize stability during peak brain development. You made a commitment together; honor the neurological window.
2. **Map your child's developmental stage** against the risk windows: 0–3 (highest risk), 11–14 (second highest), college transition (underestimated risk). Time major disruptions to the most stable windows (roughly ages 6–11 or post-14).
3. **Tell your children together, in a calm and emotionally regulated state.** Agree on the narrative beforehand. Choose a time that does not overlap with exams, birthdays, major holidays, or social stressors.
4. **Never say you wish you had never met the other parent.** Children need to know they were born from love, even imperfect or past love. The language: "We loved each other and we made you, and we will always love you."
5. **Establish a primary residence with the primary attachment figure** (usually the mother for infants). The non-primary parent should have maximum access — daily if geography allows — but overnights should be introduced gradually, especially under age three.
6. **Avoid the 2-3-2 schedule.** It trades the illusion of fairness for the reality of instability. Children should sleep in the same bed, in the same home, with the same routines during school weeks.
7. **Consider nesting for the first year post-divorce**: children stay in the family home; parents rotate in and out. This minimizes disruption during the most volatile adjustment period.
8. **Live near each other.** Geographic proximity allows the non-primary parent to participate in school drop-offs, dinners, routines, and practice — without requiring disruptive overnight transfers.
9. **Get individual therapy** to prevent "leaking" emotional pain onto children. Children should not become emotional containers for their parents' grief.
10. **Arrange child therapy as well.** Children need a neutral, safe space to process feelings they may not feel comfortable bringing to either parent.
11. **Never allow one parent to obstruct access, badmouth the other parent, or use the child as a messenger or emotional proxy.** These behaviors constitute alienation and cause measurable psychological harm.
12. **Frame sacrifice as investment.** The parent who gives more now — whether by not pressing for overnights, living closer, or adjusting schedules — earns a deeper, more trusting relationship with their child over a lifetime.

### For Adults Reflecting on Their Own Attachment Histories

1. **Identify your attachment style** (secure, anxious, avoidant, disorganized) and recognize that it was shaped pre-verbally, before memory, and is therefore not a moral failure.
2. **Seek therapy** not just in crisis but proactively, particularly if you recognize patterns of neurotic repetition — choosing partners or creating dynamics that mirror early attachment wounds.
3. **Understand that guilt is a signal, not a pathology.** A manageable level of guilt about parenting choices is your superego functioning correctly. Lean into it rather than suppressing it.
4. **Interrogate the metrics you've been given for success.** Career title, salary, followers, and status are observable but hollow. The hidden metrics — relational depth, emotional security, meaning — are what Aristotle's deathbed question actually measures.

### Research Areas Worth Exploring

- **Strange Situation Studies (Mary Ainsworth, Beatrice Beebe)** — The foundational experimental paradigm for measuring attachment security; longitudinal data going back to the 1960s directly supports Komisar's clinical claims.
- **Epigenetics and early stress exposure** — How cortisol exposure in utero and infancy produces lasting genetic expression changes; researchers at Columbia University cited in the transcript are working at this frontier.
- **Salivary cortisol studies in daycare settings** — Peer-reviewed research measuring cortisol spikes in infants during institutional daycare hours; provides empirical grounding for Komisar's strongest claims.
- **Serotonin transporter gene (5-HTTLPR) and differential susceptibility** — The "sensitivity gene" Komisar references; research by Belsky and others on how the same gene produces either heightened resilience *or* heightened vulnerability depending on environment.
- **Robert Plomin's behavioral genetics work** — For a counterpoint and complement on nature vs. nurture; *Blueprint* (2018) is his most accessible recent summary, and the tension between his genetic determinism and Komisar's environmental emphasis is genuinely productive.
- **Judith Wallerstein's divorce research** — Her landmark longitudinal study on divorce's long-term effects on children; Komisar both acknowledges and refines her findings.
- **Mark Friedman on intergenerational living** — Referenced but not fully named in the transcript; his work on the dissolution of extended family structures as a driver of mental health decline is directly relevant.
- **Susan Venker on gender role reversal and marital outcomes** — Explores how female primary breadwinner dynamics affect sexual dynamics, marital satisfaction, and relational stability.

### Related Thinkers & Practitioners

- **Adam Lane Smith** — Attachment specialist mentioned by the host; bridges attachment theory and practical relationship coaching for modern audiences.
- **David Brooks** — Cited by Komisar on the shift from communal to individualistic social structures; his book *The Second Mountain* directly addresses this theme.
- **Fred Rogers (Mr. Rogers)** — Referenced for his foundational work in child emotional literacy: *"When feelings are mentionable, they're manageable."* His entire body of work embodies Komisar's principles in practice.
- **Penelope Leach** — Child developmentalist cited by Komisar; her books on child-centered parenting are a natural companion text.

### Policy & Structural Context

- **FMLA (Family and Medical Leave Act, USA)** — Provides 12 weeks of *unpaid* job protection; no federal *paid* leave exists, making the US an outlier among developed nations.
- **Australian and UK parental leave policy** — Both countries have been more receptive to Komisar's framework at the policy level; Australia is exploring family stipends and income splitting as alternatives to institutional daycare subsidies.
- **Income splitting proposals** — The conservative policy idea (referenced in the Australian context) of treating a stay-at-home spouse as earning half of the household income for pension and tax purposes; directly addresses the financial disenfranchisement of caregiving.

---

> **Core Takeaway:** Komisar's framework is not anti-work or anti-women — it is anti-pretense. The pretense that children are resilient by default, that quality time substitutes for presence, that 50/50 custody is equitable, and that career ambition and early caregiving can be fully concurrent without cost to the child. The science she marshals is decades old and extensively replicated. What is new is the social permission — or lack thereof — to say it plainly.
