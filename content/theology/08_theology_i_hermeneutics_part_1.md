# Theology I: Hermeneutics (part 1)

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this, you should be able to:

1. **Distinguish** between the five canonical criteria (orthodoxy/rule of faith, apostolicity, internal consistency, external verification, early church recognition) and explain what specific problem each criterion is designed to solve.
2. **Reconstruct** the logical syllogism connecting inspiration → inerrancy → infallibility → authority, and articulate why the chain collapses if any single link is denied.
3. **Evaluate** Peter Enns's argument about Jesus and Mosaic authorship by identifying the precise logical options it forces and explaining why each option is theologically untenable.
4. **Distinguish** between inerrancy and infallibility as complementary but non-identical claims — specifying which is an objective description and which is an assertion about incapacity.
5. **Apply** the concept of illumination to explain why an unsaved scholar can master Hebrew grammar yet still fail to "understand" the Psalms in the sense Paul intends in 1 Corinthians 2.
6. **Articulate** the difference between *sola scriptura* as the Reformers meant it and the caricature that tradition is worthless — and explain what is at stake in that distinction.

## Structured Notes

### Overview: The Doctrine of Revelation

**Core idea:** All Christian theology rests on the prior question of how God communicates — and in what form that communication reaches us reliably.

The course is situated within the broader doctrine of **revelation**, which divides into two kinds:

- **Natural revelation** — God discloses himself through (1) *creation* and (2) *conscience*
- **Special revelation** — God discloses himself through (1) *Scripture* and (2) *Christ*

These two categories are not rivals; they are complementary. But special revelation is what makes the content of the gospel knowable. Everything we know of Christ, we know through Scripture — including the Old Testament, which Luke 24 shows points to Christ through the Law, the Prophets, and the Writings.

> **Key term — Natural Revelation:** God's self-disclosure through the created order and the moral conscience, available to all human beings.

> **Key term — Special Revelation:** God's self-disclosure through Scripture and the person of Jesus Christ, necessary for knowing the gospel.

---

### Canon: Definition and Scope

**Core idea:** "Canon" means a straight measuring rod — it names the collection of books that constitute the authoritative Bible.

> **Key term — Canon:** From the Greek *kanon* ("measuring rod" or "rule"); the recognized collection of books that constitute Holy Scripture.

The Old Testament is organized into three sections, recognized as such by the time of Christ:

1. **The Law** (Torah)
2. **The Prophets** (Nevi'im)
3. **The Writings** (Ketuvim)

By approximately **125–170 AD**, a solidified canon of both Old and New Testaments was recognized by the church.

The ordering of the canon is not arbitrary. In the English Bible: Law → History → Poetry → Prophets, ending with Malachi — leaving an open eschatological question. In the Hebrew ordering: Law → Prophets → Writings, ending with Chronicles and Cyrus's decree to return to the land — another deliberate cliffhanger. Both orderings drive the reader toward the New Testament as resolution.

---

### Canonical Criteria: How We Know What Belongs

**Core idea:** Inclusion in the canon is not arbitrary; it is determined by a convergence of criteria that together constitute a weight of evidence.

#### 1. Orthodoxy / Rule of Faith (Historicity)

> **Key term — Rule of Faith:** A concept associated with Irenaeus (2nd century AD); the principle that there is a consistent metanarrative running from Genesis to Revelation, and that no part of genuine Scripture contradicts that overarching story.

Scripture does not contradict Scripture. The entire Bible affirms a unified narrative. Any book that disrupts or contradicts this meta-story fails on this criterion. The rule of faith is not external to the text — it is the internal coherence of the text itself.

#### 2. Apostolicity

> **Key term — Apostolicity:** The criterion that canonical books were written by apostles or prophets, or by those in close authoritative relationship to them.

The theological basis is **Ephesians 2:20** — "built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, Christ Jesus himself being the cornerstone." The foundation available to us now *is* their writings.

**Important nuance:** Mark and Luke are not apostles, yet both are canonical. The justification:

- Mark is closely associated with **Peter** (an apostle)
- Luke is closely associated with **Paul** (an apostle)

Their proximity to apostolic authority satisfies the criterion. Similarly, **Hebrews** (whose authorship is uncertain — possibly Apollos) is judged by its christological content, its correspondence to the rule of faith, and its affirmation by the very early church.

#### 3. Internal Consistency + External Verification

**Internal consistency** means the Bible tells a story with integrity — no fatal self-contradictions in its doctrinal or narrative content.

The lecturer's anecdote is pedagogically significant: a student arrived with a three-ring binder of alleged contradictions sourced from a YouTube video. Working through them over months revealed that:

- Some objections were straightforwardly mistaken
- Some required careful research
- None ultimately broke the internal coherence of the text

**On scribal variation:** The Hebrew words for "1,000" and "10,000" differ by a minuscule letter — the kind of copying variation that textual criticism is designed to address. Such variants:

- Appear in a small minority of manuscripts (roughly 0.7–0.8% divergence overall)
- Never affect a doctrinal claim
- Are typically matters of numbers, definite/indefinite articles, or minor grammatical forms

**External verification** comes from archaeology and manuscript evidence:

- **Dead Sea Scrolls** (discovered in Qumran, late 1940s) confirm the antiquity and stability of Old Testament texts
- **New Testament manuscripts:** nearly 6,000 Greek manuscripts exist — compared to single-digit copies of Homer's *Iliad* or Thucydides' *Peloponnesian War*, which are accepted without question as reliable ancient texts

> **Key term — Textual Criticism:** The scholarly discipline of comparing manuscripts to reconstruct the most accurate version of an ancient text and identify variations introduced through copying.

#### 4. Early Church Recognition

Lesser in weight than the above but significant: canonical books were cited as authoritative by early church fathers from very early on. This broad, early consensus across the church is evidence of the books' recognized standing.

#### 5. Providential Preservation (Implicit)

> **Key term — Illumination:** The work of the Holy Spirit by which those who seek God are enabled to discern the spiritual authority and meaning of Scripture (2 Peter 1:20–21; 1 Corinthians 2:10–14).

Drawing on 2 Peter 1:20–21 — "men wrote as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit" — the argument is that the same Spirit who inspired the text would providentially ensure that the church receives what God intended. This is theologically implicit, not empirically demonstrable, but consistent with the doctrine of God.

### The Apocrypha and Pseudepigrapha

> **Key term — Apocrypha:** A collection of Jewish writings from roughly 400 BC–100 AD, included in the Roman Catholic Bible but excluded from the Protestant canon; they do not meet the same convergence of canonical criteria.

> **Key term — Pseudepigrapha:** Ancient Jewish and Christian writings falsely attributed to biblical figures; clearly outside the canon.

---

### History of Views on Scripture

**Core idea:** How the church understands scriptural authority has shifted significantly across historical eras, with real theological consequences at each turn.

#### Patristic Era (Church Fathers)

Scripture is affirmed as inspired by God — the very word of God, submitted to without reservation.

#### Medieval Period

Scripture remains authoritative, but tradition — councils, creeds, and eventually papal declarations — gradually rises in parallel authority. In the Roman Catholic Church, Scripture and Tradition are placed on **equal footing** as sources of authority.

#### Reformation: Sola Scriptura

> **Key term — Sola Scriptura:** Latin: "Scripture alone." The Reformation principle that Scripture is the *primary and final* authority in theology. Critically: it does not mean tradition is worthless — tradition is a useful guardrail — but that Scripture sits *above* tradition in authority.

**What sola scriptura does NOT mean:** The Bible alone and nothing else matters. Tradition, creeds, and councils have genuine value as guides.
**What it DOES mean:** When tradition and Scripture conflict, Scripture wins.

#### Modern/Enlightenment Era (1750–Present)

The Enlightenment introduced radical skepticism about revealed texts, producing several influential movements:

**Friedrich Schleiermacher** — "the father of modern Christian liberalism"

- The Bible is fallible, contains errors, reflects human religious experience
- Scripture should adapt to culture; culture shapes interpretation
- **Problem:** If Scripture is culturally relative and perpetually changing, it has no stable authority — interpretation becomes entirely subjective, located in the interpreter rather than the text

**Karl Barth and Neo-Orthodoxy**

- The Bible *becomes* the Word of God in the encounter — when it is read or preached and encountered existentially, it becomes revelation
- **Problem:** This makes revelation subjective and occasional, not objective and fixed. The Reformational claim is that the Bible *is* objectively the Word of God, regardless of anyone's subjective encounter with it

### The Peter Enns Case Study: A Full Chain of Reasoning

**Core idea:** Denying that Jesus made historically binding claims about Old Testament authorship forces one into a logical dilemma with catastrophic theological consequences.

**The argument Enns makes:**

1. Modern scholars acknowledge that the Pentateuch contains "updating" by later editors
2. Jesus attributes the Pentateuch to Moses (John 5:46–47)
3. But this doesn't bind us, because Jesus was simply "reflecting the tradition he inherited as a first-century Jew" — i.e., he was operating within cultural assumptions, not making authoritative historical judgments

**Step 1 — What this requires us to conclude about Jesus:**
There are exactly two logical options if Jesus was wrong about Mosaic authorship:

*Option A: Jesus lied.*

- He knew the Pentateuch was not by Moses but said it was anyway
- Lying is a sin (Exodus 20; the character of God in Scripture)
- A sinning Jesus is not a morally perfect Savior
- A morally imperfect Savior cannot atone for sin → salvation collapses

*Option B: Jesus was ignorant.*

- He genuinely did not know the Pentateuch's actual authorship
- But omniscience is an essential divine attribute — God knows all things past, present, and future
- An ignorant Jesus is not fully God
- A Jesus who is not fully God cannot provide infinite atonement → salvation collapses again

**Step 2 — What this does to scriptural authority:**
If Jesus' statements about Scripture can be dismissed as culturally conditioned Jewish tradition rather than authoritative judgment, then the same move can be applied to everything Jesus says. The grounds for trusting any of his claims evaporate.

**Step 3 — The appeal to Moses undermines Enns's own position:**
John 5:46–47: "If you believed Moses, you would believe me, for he wrote about me. But if you do not believe his writings, how will you believe my words?" Jesus is making Moses' writings the *basis* for belief in himself. Undermining Moses' authorship therefore undermines the textual basis for trusting Jesus's own claims — Enns cannot separate them.

**Step 4 — The contradiction in Enns's method:**
Enns wants to affirm Jesus as the incarnate Son of God while simultaneously dismissing Jesus's explicit statements about Scripture as culturally inherited assumptions. These two moves are incompatible: a fully divine Son of God who casually misstates historical facts and doesn't care is not the Jesus described in the Gospels — particularly a Jesus who in Matthew 23 openly and aggressively confronts the traditions of the Pharisees when they contradict Scripture.

### Defining the Properties of Scripture

**Core idea:** Inspiration, inerrancy, infallibility, authority, necessity, sufficiency, clarity, and illumination are distinct but interconnected properties — each follows logically from the others.

> **Key term — Inspiration:** The process by which God, through the Holy Spirit, worked through human authors — using their personalities, idioms, and styles — to produce the words he intended. The result is that the words are genuinely human *and* genuinely divine (2 Peter 1:20–21).

> **Key term — Inerrancy:** The objective property of Scripture that it contains no errors in its original manuscripts. Carl Henry's syllogism: *Scripture comes from God. God cannot lie, deceive, mislead, or err. Therefore Scripture does not lie, deceive, mislead, or err.*

> **Key term — Infallibility:** The claim that Scripture is *incapable* of erring — not merely that it happens to contain no errors, but that by its nature it cannot. **Distinction from inerrancy:** Inerrancy is an objective description (the Bible has no errors); infallibility is an assertion about inherent incapacity (the Bible cannot err). They are complementary: one describes the result, the other describes the nature.

> **Key term — Authority:** Because Scripture is inspired, inerrant, and infallible, it stands *over* the reader — not the reverse. To disbelieve or disobey any word of Scripture is to disbelieve or disobey God. The reader is in submission to the text.

> **Key term — Necessity:** Scripture is necessary for (a) knowing the gospel, (b) maintaining spiritual life, and (c) knowing God's will. These things cannot be known through natural revelation alone.

> **Key term — Sufficiency:** Scripture is adequate — fully enough — for both doctrine and life, as appropriated through the Spirit (2 Peter 1:3–4). Nothing needs to be added to it.

> **Key term — Clarity (Perspicuity):** The Bible is understandable for those who read it with the Spirit's help and a genuine willingness to follow it. Not all passages are equally clear, but the Bible's overall message — sin, judgment, repentance, salvation by grace — is accessible. This does not mean all interpretation is easy; it means the essential message is not hidden.

> **Key term — Illumination:** The Spirit's work of enabling readers to discern the spiritual authority and meaning of Scripture. Basis: 1 Corinthians 2:10, 14. Distinction: an unsaved scholar *can* understand the grammar, syntax, and literary structure of a biblical text. What they cannot discern is its spiritual authority over them — they see it as literature (like the Aeneid), not as binding revelation from God. The problem is not intellectual but spiritual.

### The "4 C's" Framework

**Core idea:** Canon, confession, covenants, and Christ form an interlocking framework for reading the whole Bible.

- **Canon** — the collection of books: Law, Prophets, Writings, Gospels, Acts, Epistles, Revelation
- **Confession** — what we affirm about that canon: inspired, inerrant, infallible, authoritative, clear, necessary, sufficient
- **Covenants** — the structural backbone of the Bible's narrative: Adam → Noah → Abraham → Moses → David → New Covenant; each pointing forward
- **Christ/Messiah** — all covenants and all of the Old Testament point toward and find their fulfillment in Jesus

Reading a passage well requires three levels of context:

1. What does this passage mean?
2. What does it mean within its *book*?
3. What does it mean within the *whole Bible*?

## Concept Map Connections

- `Natural Revelation` → is a necessary but insufficient precursor to → `Special Revelation` *(natural revelation discloses God's existence and moral character; special revelation alone discloses the gospel)*
- `Inspiration` → logically entails → `Inerrancy` *(if God is the ultimate author, and God cannot err, the text cannot err)*
- `Inerrancy` → is distinguished from but entails → `Infallibility` *(inerrancy = objective description of actual errorlessness; infallibility = claim about inherent incapacity to err — one describes the result, the other the nature)*
- `Inerrancy` → grounds → `Authority` *(a text with errors can be questioned and overruled; only an error-free text can stand as an unconditional authority over the reader)*
- `Apostolicity` → enables → `Canonical Recognition` *(connection to apostolic authorship or close apostolic association is the primary human criterion for inclusion)*
- `Rule of Faith` → functions as → `Internal Consistency Criterion` *(a book that contradicts the Bible's metanarrative fails the orthodoxy test and is excluded)*
- `Sola Scriptura` → is distinguished from → `Medieval Scripture + Tradition` *(sola scriptura does not reject tradition but subordinates it; medieval Catholicism placed them on equal authority)*
- `Illumination` → is required for → `Discernment of Authority` *(even technically expert readers cannot perceive Scripture as binding divine revelation without the Spirit's work — grammar ≠ spiritual discernment)*
- `Textual Criticism` → supports → `Inerrancy` *(the ~99.2–99.3% manuscript agreement, and the minor, doctrinally irrelevant nature of variations, reinforces confidence in the transmitted text)*
- `Covenants` → progressively converge on → `Christ/Messiah` *(each covenant — Adamic, Noahic, Abrahamic, Mosaic, Davidic, New — functions as a step in a trajectory culminating in Jesus)*

## Common Misconceptions

### 1. Misconception: Sola scriptura means only the Bible counts; all tradition is rejected

**Why it's tempting:** The phrase "Scripture *alone*" sounds absolute. The Reformation was explicitly a break from Roman Catholic tradition, which makes it natural to assume the Reformers were rejecting tradition wholesale.

**Why it's wrong and what the correction teaches:** The Reformers — Luther, Calvin, and others — read the church fathers voraciously and appealed to them constantly. Sola scriptura was never anti-tradition; it was anti-*tradition-as-equal-authority*. The precise claim is that when Scripture and tradition conflict, Scripture is the final arbiter. Tradition functions as a guardrail, a commentary, a record of how the church has read the text — valuable precisely because it is not authoritative in itself. Collapsing this distinction leads either to Rome (tradition = Scripture) or to a naïve individualism where each reader starts from scratch — neither of which was the Reformation's intent.

---

### 2. Misconception: Inerrancy and infallibility mean the same thing

**Why it's tempting:** In ordinary conversation, "without error" and "incapable of error" are used interchangeably. Both sound like strong, positive claims about the Bible's reliability.

**Why it's wrong and what the correction teaches:** The distinction is subtle but theologically important. *Inerrancy* is an external, objective description: examining the text, we find it contains no errors. *Infallibility* is a claim about the Bible's nature and disposition: it is incapable of leading astray, by virtue of what it is. You can imagine a text that happens to contain no errors (inerrancy satisfied) but that could in principle have contained them. Infallibility goes further and says the text, because of its divine origin, is constitutionally incapable of error. The two concepts reinforce each other but address different questions — one describes the product, the other the process and nature.

---

### 3. Misconception: Scribal errors in manuscripts disprove inerrancy

**Why it's tempting:** We know the Bible was copied by human hands thousands of times over centuries. Human beings make mistakes. If the copies have errors, doesn't that mean the Bible has errors?

**Why it's wrong and what the correction teaches:** Inerrancy applies to the *original manuscripts* (the autographs), not to every copy ever made. Textual criticism exists precisely to identify and correct copying variations — and the astonishing point is that across nearly 6,000 New Testament manuscripts, the agreement rate is approximately 99.2–99.3%. Where variations exist, they are minor (numbers, articles, word order) and never affect a doctrinal claim. The sheer volume of manuscripts makes the Bible the *most textually verifiable* ancient document in existence — far better attested than Homer or Thucydides, which no one doubts. The misconception mistakes the copy for the original and ignores the discipline designed to handle exactly this problem.

---

### 4. Misconception: Illumination means uneducated readers understand the Bible better than scholars

**Why it's tempting:** The lecturer says an unsaved PhD in Semitics cannot truly "understand" the Psalms, while a Spirit-filled believer can. This sounds like it devalues scholarship and elevates simple faith.

**Why it's wrong and what the correction teaches:** Paul's point in 1 Corinthians 2 is about *spiritual discernment*, not grammatical or literary comprehension. The unsaved scholar can and does understand the text's linguistic, literary, and historical content — often better than untrained believers. What they cannot perceive is the text's *authority over them* — that these are not merely poems or wisdom literature but the living word of a God to whom they are accountable. A Spirit-filled believer with poor Greek still grasps that the Psalms are speaking to them as divine address. Illumination is about perceiving the ontological status of the text, not about bypassing the hard work of careful reading. The two — scholarly rigor and spiritual discernment — are not rivals; ideally they work together.

---

### 5. Misconception: "The Bible is self-authenticating" means we can't use external evidence to support it

**Why it's tempting:** The term "self-authenticating" sounds like it means the Bible proves itself by itself alone, making archaeology, manuscripts, and external evidence irrelevant or circular.

**Why it's wrong and what the correction teaches:** Self-authentication means the Bible does not derive its authority from an external human institution that grants it canonical status — the church did not *create* the canon, it *recognized* what was already authoritative. But this is entirely compatible with external evidence *confirming* what the text already claims about itself. The Dead Sea Scrolls, manuscript counts, archaeological corroboration — these don't give the Bible its authority, but they function as corroborating witnesses. The distinction matters because it prevents two errors: (a) thinking the church's imprimatur makes Scripture authoritative (Roman Catholic error), and (b) thinking we need archaeological proof before we can trust Scripture (an evidentialist overcorrection). External evidence is confirmation, not foundation.

Then walk through each of the two options that claim forces upon us (what they are, why each is theologically untenable, and what each destroys). 2. **Without looking at your notes, explain the five criteria for canonicity in sequence.** For each one: state what the criterion is, give its theological or evidential basis, and identify what problem it is designed to solve (i.e., what kind of book would fail it). An answer that lists names without explaining the logic of each criterion is incomplete.

3. **Without looking at your notes, distinguish inspiration, inerrancy, infallibility, authority, and illumination — not by defining them in isolation, but by explaining how each one follows from or depends on the previous one.** The chain should move logically: because A is true, B follows; because B is true, C follows. Then add illumination and explain why it is a different *kind* of claim from the others — it is about the reader's capacity, not the text's properties. A complete answer shows the logical dependency chain and correctly places illumination as categorically distinct.
