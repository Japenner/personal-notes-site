# Bible and the Gospel | What is the Biblical Gospel? (Part 1)

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this material, you should be able to:

1. **Articulate** a Biblical understanding of the gospel by identifying its defining characteristics — that it is a historical message, focused on Christ, grounded in Scripture, about salvation, and demanding a response of faith — drawing specifically from 1 Corinthians 15 and Romans 1.
2. **Distinguish** between what the gospel *is* (a message with specific content) and what the gospel *requires* (a response of faith), explaining why the distinction matters for both preaching and personal belief.
3. **Evaluate** a proposed definition of the gospel against the biblical criteria identified in the course texts, identifying which elements are present, absent, or distorted.
4. **Reconstruct** the logical structure of Paul's argument in 1 Corinthians 15:1–8 — why he lists the eyewitnesses, what that listing is meant to establish, and how it connects to the nature of the gospel as historical.
5. **Apply** the course's understanding of spiritual disciplines (Word, prayer, local church engagement) to describe what a robustly Christian life looks like in practice, using David Mathis's three-category framework.
6. **Articulate the relationship** between beholding Christ through Scripture (2 Corinthians 3:18) and personal spiritual transformation, explaining the mechanism the text proposes.

## Structured Notes

### Course Overview and Objectives

**Core idea:** This course is designed to produce students who understand the gospel, practice biblically grounded spiritual disciplines, and engage the local church — all oriented around growing in joy and transformation in Christ.

The course covers four major areas in sequence:
1. The biblical understanding of the gospel and the new life it produces
2. Biblically oriented spiritual disciplines (using *Habits of Grace* by David Mathis)
3. The character and content of the Bible (doctrine of Scripture; using *The Story of Scripture* by Emerson)
4. The proper interpretation of Scripture (using *Dig Deeper*)

The textbooks are deliberately integrated: the Bible is the primary textbook; the supplementary books serve the four content areas.

> **Key term — Perspicuity:** The clarity of Scripture — the doctrine that the Bible, in its essential content, can be understood by ordinary readers without specialized training. Ironically, "perspicuity" is itself an unclear word, which the lecturer notes with humor.

> **Key term — Inerrancy:** The doctrine that the Bible, as originally given, is without error in all that it affirms. (Mentioned as a term most students will already know, contrasted with perspicuity as a less familiar term.)

> **Key term — Spiritual Disciplines:** Structured, intentional practices that orient a believer's life toward growth in Christ. Mathis organizes these into three categories: engagement with the Word, engagement in prayer, and engagement in the local church.

#### The Role of the Local Church

The lecturer makes a specific, emphatic point: Cedarville University is not the local church and does not intend to replace it. The explicit goal of the Bible minor is to produce "exceptional local church members." This is not incidental — it shapes what the course is trying to do. Normal Christian life, in this framework, is life embedded in a local congregation.

#### Two Anchoring Scriptures for the Course's Aims

**Matthew 13:44 — The Hidden Treasure**

Jesus describes the Kingdom of Heaven as a treasure hidden in a field. A man finds it, covers it back up, and then — *in his joy* — sells everything he has to buy the field.

The pedagogical point the lecturer draws from this: the man does not sell everything begrudgingly. The manner is *joy*. This is how the lecturer wants students to approach Christ and his Word — not as an obligation but as a response to something whose value is genuinely greater than everything else. The affective goal of the course is that students' joy in Christ would increase.

**2 Corinthians 3:17–18 — Beholding and Being Transformed**

> "We all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another."

The mechanism here is important: **beholding → transformation**. As a person sees Christ more clearly — which happens through Scripture — that person is progressively transformed into Christ's image. The course's deeper goal is not just skill acquisition but this kind of transformation. The lecturer acknowledges he cannot measure this on a test but prays toward it.

### What Is the Gospel?

**Core idea:** The gospel is a specific, historical, Christ-centered message about salvation from sin, grounded in Scripture, that demands a response of faith — and understanding it precisely is the central task of the course.

#### Why Precision Matters

The lecturer opens with a Spurgeon quotation:

> "The ultimate issues, as seen upon a dying bed, is a true test as it is an inevitable one. Preach that which will enable men to face death without fear, and you will preach nothing but the old gospel."

The point: the gospel is not merely academic content. It is the most important message in the world because it is the message that matters at the boundary of life and death. This framing is reinforced by the personal story below.

#### The Hospital Bed Story (Pedagogical Demonstration)

The lecturer recounts visiting an elderly neighbor dying in the hospital — a woman whose faith status was uncertain. With potentially only minutes and a few words available, the question becomes: *What do you say?*

He asked her what scripture had been significant in her life. She named Psalm 23. He read it. Then he pivoted: *"Let me tell you about the Good Shepherd who lays down his life for the sheep."*

The story demonstrates the stakes of the question "What is the gospel?" — if you don't know it precisely, you cannot communicate it when it matters most. The lecturer's answer in that moment was not a doctrine list; it was a christological move from a familiar text to the person and work of Jesus.

*(The lecturer notes he will return later to explain how the neighbor responded.)*

### Key Biblical Texts Defining the Gospel

#### Text 1: 1 Corinthians 15:1–8

**Context:** Paul has spent 14 chapters correcting a church full of problems — divisions, sexual immorality, lawsuits, idolatry, abuses of the Lord's Supper, confusion about spiritual gifts. As the *capstone* of the letter, he returns them to the gospel. The implication: everything else he has corrected makes sense only in light of this foundation.

> "Now I would remind you, brothers, of the gospel I preached to you, which you received, in which you stand, and by which you are being saved… For I delivered to you as of first importance what I also received: that Christ died for our sins in accordance with the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day in accordance with the Scriptures, and that he appeared to Cephas, then to the twelve. Then he appeared to more than five hundred brothers at one time, most of whom are still alive, though some have fallen asleep…" (1 Cor. 15:1–6)

**Characteristics of the gospel derived from this text:**

| Feature | Evidence in the text |
|---|---|
| **It is a message** | Paul "preached" it — proclamation language |
| **It demands a response** | They "received" it — faith is the response |
| **It is about salvation** | "By which you are being saved" |
| **It focuses on Christ** | "Christ died for our sins… was buried… was raised" |
| **It is biblical** | "In accordance with the Scriptures" (stated twice) |
| **It is historical** | The eyewitness list: Cephas, the twelve, 500+, James, Paul |

**Why the eyewitness list matters — full chain of reasoning:**

Paul does not list the eyewitnesses to be impressive. He lists them to make a specific claim: *this happened in real time and space.* The resurrection is not myth, allegory, or spiritual metaphor. It is an event that real people observed. Most of them were still alive when Paul wrote, which means his readers could have verified the claim. The historical grounding is load-bearing for the gospel: if the resurrection didn't happen, the message collapses (a point Paul makes explicitly later in chapter 15). Therefore, the gospel is not simply a set of beliefs — it is a report of events.

> **Key term — Gospel:** From the Greek *euangelion*, meaning "good news" or "good report." It is a message — something that can be proclaimed, received, and believed — about the person and work of Jesus Christ, specifically his death for sins and resurrection, which makes salvation possible.

---

#### Text 2: Romans 1:1–6

**Context:** Paul opens his most systematic letter by defining his own apostolic calling in terms of the gospel. Before he gets to any argument, he establishes what his ministry is *about*.

> "Paul, a servant of Christ Jesus, called to be an apostle, set apart for the gospel of God, which he promised beforehand through his prophets in the holy Scriptures, concerning his Son, who was descended from David according to the flesh and declared to be the Son of God in power according to the Spirit of holiness by his resurrection from the dead, Jesus Christ our Lord, through whom we have received grace and apostleship to bring about the obedience of faith for the sake of his name among all the nations, including you who are called to belong to Jesus Christ…" (Rom. 1:1–6)

**Characteristics confirmed and extended from this text:**

| Feature | Evidence in the text |
|---|---|
| **It is a message** | Paul is "set apart for the gospel" — it is the content of his ministry |
| **It is biblical** | "Promised beforehand through his prophets in the holy Scriptures" |
| **It focuses on Christ** | The entire passage is a description of Jesus — his lineage, his identity, his resurrection |
| **It is about salvation** | "Through whom we have received grace" |
| **It demands a response** | "The obedience of faith" |
| **It is for all nations** | "Among all the nations, including you" |

**New features this text adds:**

1. **The Davidic lineage:** Jesus is "descended from David according to the flesh." This is not incidental. It connects Jesus to the Old Testament covenant with David (2 Samuel 7), in which God promised that a son of David would reign forever. The gospel is not a new story — it is the fulfillment of a very old promise. The lecturer notes this will be developed later when the course covers biblical covenants.

2. **The scope — all nations:** The gospel is not ethnically or geographically restricted. It is for "all the nations." The course objective about engagement in God's mission of reaching the nations connects directly here.

3. **God's glory as the goal:** The response of faith is "for the sake of his name." The gospel is not ultimately about human benefit alone — it is oriented toward the glory of God.

> **Key term — Obedience of Faith:** Paul's phrase in Romans 1:5 (and 16:26) describing the proper response to the gospel. It encompasses both the act of believing and the life of obedience that flows from that belief — faith that is not passive but that expresses itself in a transformed life.

**Connecting Romans 1:16:**
> "I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is the power of God for salvation to everyone who believes."

This verse — mentioned briefly at the close of the lecture — adds two things: (1) the gospel is *powerful*, not merely informative; it actually accomplishes salvation; (2) the scope is "everyone who believes," consistent with the "all nations" language above.

### Emerging Definition of the Gospel

After these two texts, the following characteristics have been established:

- **A message** — it can be proclaimed, preached, and reported
- **Biblical** — grounded in and anticipated by the Old Testament Scriptures
- **Christ-focused** — centered on the person, lineage, death, burial, and resurrection of Jesus
- **About salvation** — addressing the problem of sin and making right relationship with God possible
- **Historical** — rooted in real, verifiable, space-and-time events
- **Demanding a response** — the proper reception is faith ("obedience of faith")
- **Universal in scope** — for all nations, everyone who believes
- **For God's glory** — oriented toward his name, not merely human benefit

## Socratic Questions

### Comprehension

1. According to the two texts examined (1 Corinthians 15 and Romans 1), what are the defining characteristics of the gospel? Can you state each one and identify the specific phrase in the text that establishes it — not just assert that the text "talks about" the gospel?

2. The lecturer says the gospel "demands a response." What specifically is that response, and how do both texts name it? Is the response the same in both texts, or do the texts emphasize different aspects of it?

3. What is the difference between *perspicuity* and *inerrancy* as doctrines of Scripture? Why does knowing the distinction matter for understanding what the Bible claims about itself?

### Analysis

4. Paul lists more than five hundred eyewitnesses in 1 Corinthians 15 and notes that most are still alive. What is the logical function of this detail? What would be lost from the gospel claim if Paul had not included it? What specific feature of the gospel does it underwrite?

5. The lecturer describes the gospel as both "biblical" (grounded in Old Testament scripture) and "historical" (groted in real, verifiable events). Are these the same claim? Could something be biblical but not historical, or historical but not biblical? How do these two features relate to each other in 1 Corinthians 15?

6. In Romans 1:5, Paul says his apostolic mission is to bring about "the obedience of faith." If faith were simply intellectual agreement with true propositions about Jesus, would this phrase make sense? What does this phrase imply about the nature of the faith that the gospel demands — and how does that complicate a purely cognitive definition of belief?

7. The lecturer draws on Matthew 13:44 to make an affective point — the man sells everything *in his joy*. But is this passage actually about the gospel? What does the Kingdom of Heaven mean in Matthew's Gospel, and does the lecturer's use of the passage hold up under scrutiny? What would it take to evaluate whether this application is legitimate?

8. The course objective says students should be able to "evaluate articulations of the gospel." What criteria would you use to evaluate whether a proposed definition of the gospel is adequate? Which criteria come from 1 Corinthians 15, which from Romans 1, and which (if any) would require other texts?

9. The lecturer says Paul returns to the gospel as the *capstone* of 1 Corinthians after fourteen chapters of corrections. What does the structural placement of 1 Corinthians 15 suggest about the relationship between the gospel and Christian ethics or church behavior? Is the gospel a *foundation* for behavior, a *correction* to misbehavior, or both — and what's the difference?

### Extension

10. The lecturer's neighbor responded to Psalm 23 as a personally significant text. He used it as a bridge to the gospel ("Let me tell you about the Good Shepherd who lays down his life for the sheep"). Is this a legitimate move exegetically? What assumptions about the relationship between the Old Testament and the New Testament does it require? Could the same move be made with any Psalm, or does it require specific content in the text?

11. Paul says the gospel was "promised beforehand through the prophets in the holy Scriptures" (Romans 1:2). This implies the Old Testament already contains the gospel in some form. But the Old Testament does not explicitly name Jesus or describe the crucifixion. How far does the continuity go? At what point does "the gospel was anticipated in the Old Testament" shade into over-reading Old Testament texts — and how would you identify that line?

12. The lecturer lists "for God's glory" as a feature of the gospel from Romans 1:5 ("for the sake of his name"). But 1 Corinthians 15 does not emphasize God's glory explicitly — it emphasizes sin, death, resurrection, and appearances. Is "for God's glory" a feature of the gospel itself, or is it a *consequence* of the gospel? Does the distinction matter for how you would define or preach the gospel?

13. The 2 Corinthians 3:18 mechanism is: beholding Christ through Scripture → transformation into Christ's image. The lecturer presents this as the spiritual logic underlying the course. But a student could engage Scripture extensively — reading, studying, even memorizing — without being transformed. What does the text itself suggest is required for beholding to produce transformation? Does the mechanism work automatically, or are there conditions?

14. The lecturer emphasizes that the gospel is universal — "for all nations, everyone who believes." At the same time, the response required is personal — individual faith. How do you hold together the universal scope of the gospel's offer with the particular, individual nature of the required response? Is there a tension here, and if so, is it resolved in the texts examined?

15. The course explicitly frames the local church as the *destination* of students — the goal is to produce "exceptional local church members." But many students will come from or attend churches where the gospel is preached imprecisely or incompletely. If a student now has a more precise biblical definition of the gospel than their local church articulates, what obligation (if any) does that create? Is loyalty to the local church in tension with fidelity to a precise gospel?

## Common Misconceptions

### 1. Misconception: "The gospel" means the Bible, or specifically the four Gospels (Matthew, Mark, Luke, John)

**Why it's tempting:** In common English usage, "the gospels" refers to the first four books of the New Testament — it's a genre label. Non-Christians often use "the gospel" to mean "the Christian book" or "the Bible in general." This is the answer several people gave in the lecturer's street canvassing.

**Why it's wrong and what the correction reveals:** The gospel is not a text — it is a *message*, a specific announcement with definable content. The four Gospels are called gospels because they narrate the events the gospel message announces; they are the vehicle, not the message itself. Paul preaches "the gospel" in 1 Corinthians 15 and defines it in a few sentences: Christ died for our sins, was buried, was raised. This message *can* be communicated in many forms — a few sentences, a letter, a narrative book, a spoken proclamation. The distinction matters because if the gospel is the Bible in general, you cannot *summarize* it or *articulate* it — you can only gesture at a book. But Paul's point in 1 Corinthians 15 is precisely that the gospel is something specific enough to state, receive, and stand in.

---

### 2. Misconception: The gospel is primarily a set of instructions for how to be a good person or how to get to heaven through effort

**Why it's tempting:** This is what many people assume Christianity teaches — a moral system, a set of rules, a pathway of self-improvement. It's also the most natural human religious instinct: do something to earn something. When people on the street said "how to get to heaven," they were gesturing at this.

**Why it's wrong and what the correction reveals:** The gospel in 1 Corinthians 15 is not about what you must do but about what Christ *has done* — past tense, accomplished, finished. "Christ died for our sins… was buried… was raised." The gospel is a *report*, not an instruction. The response it demands (faith) is precisely the opposite of earning — it is receiving what has already been accomplished by another. The distinction matters enormously in practice: a moral-effort framework produces either pride (if you think you're succeeding) or despair (if you realize you're not). The gospel produces neither — it produces dependence and gratitude, which is what Paul's "in his joy he sold everything" imagery captures.

---

### 3. Misconception: The resurrection is a spiritual or metaphorical truth, not a bodily historical event

**Why it's tempting:** Modern audiences are often uncomfortable with claims about physical bodies rising from the dead. It's easier to interpret "resurrection" as a metaphor for hope, or a spiritual experience, or the ongoing influence of Jesus's teachings. This also sounds more intellectually sophisticated.

**Why it's wrong and what the correction reveals:** The entire point of Paul's eyewitness list in 1 Corinthians 15 is to forestall exactly this move. He names specific people — Cephas, the twelve, five hundred at once, James, himself — most of whom were *still alive* when he wrote. A spiritual or metaphorical resurrection does not require eyewitnesses; you don't need five hundred people to testify that they felt hopeful. The eyewitness list only makes sense if Paul is claiming something happened that could in principle be confirmed or disconfirmed by observers. Paul's entire argument in the rest of 1 Corinthians 15 depends on the physical reality of the resurrection — "if Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile and you are still in your sins" (15:17). A metaphorical resurrection cannot bear that weight.

---

### 4. Misconception: The response to the gospel is simply intellectual assent — believing that the facts about Jesus are true

**Why it's tempting:** The texts repeatedly use the word "believe" ("everyone who believes," "unless you believed in vain"). It's natural to interpret this as: accept the proposition that Jesus died and rose as historically true.

**Why it's wrong and what the correction reveals:** Paul's phrase in Romans 1:5 is "the obedience of faith" — not "the agreement of faith" or "the acceptance of faith." Faith, in the Pauline framework, is not a purely cognitive act. It involves reception, commitment, and a reorientation of life. The man in Matthew 13:44 doesn't just conclude intellectually that the treasure is valuable — he sells everything and acts on that conclusion. James 2:19 (not in the lecture but relevant to the distinction) notes that demons "believe" in the propositional sense and tremble. The faith the gospel demands is one that transforms the believer — which is why the course objective connects gospel to "new life," and why 2 Corinthians 3:18 describes progressive transformation as the expected outcome of genuine engagement with Christ.

---

### 5. Misconception: The gospel is a New Testament innovation — something that begins with Jesus and has no essential connection to the Old Testament

**Why it's tempting:** Jesus, the cross, and the resurrection are New Testament events. It's natural to think the gospel simply *is* the New Testament message, with the Old Testament being a separate, prior religious system.

**Why it's wrong and what the correction reveals:** Both texts examined explicitly refute this. In 1 Corinthians 15, the death and resurrection happened "in accordance with the Scriptures" — twice. In Romans 1, the gospel was "promised beforehand through his prophets in the holy Scriptures." The lecturer notes Genesis 3 already contains a promise about one who will crush the serpent's head. Jesus's Davidic lineage (Romans 1:3) connects to a covenant made with David centuries before the incarnation. The gospel is not a new story — it is the fulfillment of a story that the entire Old Testament has been telling. This matters because it means the Old Testament is not merely background or context for the real message; it is part of the gospel's own claim to coherence and truth. A gospel disconnected from the Old Testament is a different, thinner gospel.
